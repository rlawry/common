How to Use This Guide

Read this section first - it explains the strategy
This guide was built by analyzing three real NYS Earth & Space Sciences Regents exams (June 2025, August 2025, January 2026) alongside their official rating guides. The goal is simple: tell you exactly what scorers are looking for so you earn credit every time.
The Scoring Rules

The rating guide says scorers must accept any response that is "scientifically accurate and demonstrates adequate knowledge." This means:

  • You don't have to memorize exact wording - but you must use scientific reasoning.
  • If a question says to use numerical evidence, a response without numbers gets zero credit - always cite data from graphs.
  • All parts of your answer must be correct. If you write too much, you might say something false. Keep your answers SIMPLE.
  • You need to answer all parts of the question. If you are missing any part, you will receive no credit.
The Five Earth Systems - Name Them Correctly

Questions constantly ask you to identify which Earth system is affected. Use these exact names:

SystemWhat It IncludesExamples on Exam
AtmosphereAll gases surrounding EarthCO₂ levels, climate, greenhouse effect
HydrosphereAll water (oceans, rivers, ice, groundwater)Sea level rise, aquifers, ocean currents
GeosphereSolid Earth - rocks, soil, plate tectonicsErosion, mines, rock layers, volcanoes
BiosphereAll living thingsBiodiversity, species extinction, habitats
CryosphereIce on EarthGlaciers, polar regions, ice ages, Antarctica
When a question says "identify one Earth system and describe how it changes" - name a specific system AND describe a specific change to it. Both parts are required for credit.

Constructed-Response Question Stems

The exact task wording the NY HS-ESS Regents uses - and how to earn the 1 point
How CR questions are scored: On the HS-ESS exam, every constructed-response question is worth 1 credit, all-or-nothing - the rating guide states "Fractional credit is not allowed." Most stems pack two or more tasks into one sentence, joined by words like "and," "also," "explain," or "justify." You get the point only if you complete every part. Example (Jan '26 Q27): "Identify the Earth system being impacted least... and use numerical evidence to explain how this solution reduces the impact." Naming the system without the numbers - or numbers without naming the system - scores 0.
Explain & Describe Give the mechanism - what changed, and the effect it caused ▼
"Explain how [X] is a factor in [Y]."  /  "Explain the process that [causes Y]."
Cause → The actual process / mechanism → Effect
Jan '26 Q5: "The greater mass of Betelgeuse causes nucleosynthesis (fusion) to occur at a faster rate than in the Sun."
✗ "Betelgeuse is bigger" - restates the data with no mechanism = 0 credit
"Describe how [X] affects [Y]. Also describe how this change affects [Z]."  /  "...and describe how this process is responsible for [effect]."
Effect on Y + Second effect (on Z)
June '25 Q11: "Trees use photosynthesis to make food; this removes CO₂ from the air, which lowers atmospheric carbon dioxide levels."
✓ Watch for "Also..." or "and describe..." - that is a second required task, not a restatement
"Describe the conditions necessary for [event]."
Condition 1 + Condition 2
Jan '26 Q35: "Bennu and Earth must reach the point where their orbits cross at the same time."
Use the Source Pull real data, numbers, or terms from the graph / model / table - never from memory ▼
"Use evidence from the [graph/model/table] to describe..."  /  "Using evidence from the [source], construct an explanation that describes..."
State what the source shows (with data) → Connect it to the science
Jan '26 Q15: "Limestone and dolostone mines on the map line up with the major highways, so the availability of this rock shaped where roads and construction occurred."
✗ Answering from memory without referencing the source = 0 credit
"Identify the numerical value(s) of [X] from the graph."  /  "Determine, in [units], how much..."
Read the source → Report the number with its unit
Aug '25 Q32: "The Moon will be ≈ 3.8 cm farther away - too small a change to noticeably affect its period of revolution."
"Use the model to compare the [temporal/spatial] scale of X to Y."  /  "Compare X to Y."
X is greater / less / about equal to Y + Cite the source
June '25 Q39: "Earthquake subsidence happens in seconds, while ghost-forest subsidence takes years to decades."
Claim, Argument & Justify State a position, then back it with evidence - a bare claim or checkmark scores 0 ▼
"Place a check mark in the Support or Refute box. Justify your response using evidence from the information provided."
Check the box + Justify with evidence
Jan '26 Q22: "Support - Charon's rotational period (6.4 days) equals its orbital period (6.4 days), so the same side always faces Pluto."
✗ Checking the box with no justification = 0 credit
"Make a claim that describes [X]."  /  "Construct an explanation to support the claim that [X]."
State the claim + Support it with evidence from the source
Aug '25 Q33: "Claim: a total solar eclipse will become impossible. Evidence: the Moon is slowly moving away from Earth, so it will appear too small to fully cover the Sun."
✗ Claim with no supporting evidence = 0 credit
"Construct an argument, based on evidence in the model, about how [X] caused [Y]."
Claim + Evidence + Reasoning
Aug '25 Q23: "Claim: stromatolites changed Earth's early atmosphere. Evidence: they carried out photosynthesis and released O₂. Reasoning: that O₂ built up over time, creating Earth's first oxygen-rich atmosphere."
✓ All 3 parts required: claim, evidence, and reasoning
"Identify the [Earth system / vehicle / option] impacted most/least. Justify your response using numerical evidence."
Name it + Numerical support from the source
Jan '26 Q27: "Atmosphere - the Eurostar releases only 6 g CO₂/km versus 255 g/km for a flight, so it adds far less CO₂ to the atmosphere."

Topic 1: Stars, Stellar Evolution & the Big Bang

Appears on ALL THREE exams - highest priority topic
▼
H-R Diagram - What You Must Know
  • Main sequence stars run from hot/bright (upper left) to cool/dim (lower right)
  • As the Sun becomes a red giant: temperature decreases, absolute magnitude decreases (it gets brighter - remember: lower number = brighter)
  • Red giants are large, cool, bright - upper right of H-R diagram
  • White dwarfs are small, hot, dim - lower left of H-R diagram
June 2025 Q1 answer language (rated correct):
Change in relative temperature: "cooler" / "lower temperature"
Change in relative absolute magnitude: "decreased" / "lower magnitude value"
Nucleosynthesis - Mass Determines Everything
  • The mass of a star determines its lifespan AND the rate of nucleosynthesis
  • More massive stars burn fuel faster → shorter lifespan → produce heavier elements
  • Heavier elements form at higher temperatures, later in a star's life
  • The Sun fuses hydrogen → helium (main sequence)
  • More massive stars fuse helium → carbon → oxygen → neon → iron (in sequence)
  • Elements heavier than iron only form during a supernova
  • Betelgeuse has helium, carbon, oxygen, neon, etc. already → it is older/more evolved
Jan 2026 Q5 (rated correct): "The greater mass of Betelgeuse causes nucleosynthesis to occur at a faster rate than the Sun." / "The more massive a star, the greater the rate of fusion."
DO write: "The greater mass of [star] causes a faster rate of fusion/nucleosynthesis."
DON'T write: "Betelgeuse is bigger." (no mechanism = no credit)
Big Bang Theory - Three Lines of Evidence
EvidenceWhat It ShowsKey Phrase
Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) Energy left over from Big Bang, found in all directions "Energy distributed throughout the universe"; "universe cooled and became transparent"
Hubble's Law / Galaxy Recessional Velocity Galaxies farther away move faster = universe expanding "Galaxies farther from Earth have greater recessional velocities, indicating an expanding universe"
Redshift Wavelengths of light from distant galaxies are stretched longer "Greater wavelengths / red shift is evidence for the expansion of the universe"
CMBR statements the rating guide accepts (Jan 2026 Q1):
Statement 1: CMBR is "energy, predicted by scientists, that is distributed throughout the universe"
Statement 2: CMBR is detectable because the universe "cooled and then became transparent"
Statement 3: Density variations indicate "galaxies formed in areas of higher density"
Aug 2025 Q12 - Redshift answer (rated correct):
Difference in wavelength: "The wavelengths have become greater / longer" or "The wavelengths show a red shift."
How universe changed: "The change to greater wavelengths is evidence for the expansion of the universe."
Note: Your answer MUST include that the universe is "expanding/getting bigger" - just saying "wavelengths increased" is not enough.
Sunspots & Solar Irradiance
  • Sunspot cycle: approximately 11 years
  • More sunspots = higher solar irradiance (more total energy output)
  • More sunspots = higher frequency radiation emitted
  • Sunspots are regions of cooler temperatures on the Sun's surface
  • Fewer sunspots → less solar energy → inferred decrease in Earth's temperatures
Aug 2025 Q3 (rated correct): "Higher frequency radiation is emitted at times near maxima in the solar cycle" / "Solar irradiance is higher when the number of sunspots is higher."
The June 2025 exam had a trick: "An increase in solar output is associated with a decrease in sunspots" - this statement is FALSE and should NOT be checked. More sunspots = more output.

Topic 2: Climate Change, CO₂ & the Atmosphere

Appears on ALL THREE exams - multiple CR questions each time
▼
The CO₂ → Temperature Mechanism - Most Important Phrase

Every time CO₂ and temperature appear together, you must describe energy flow:

The rated-correct formula (Aug 2025 Q46):
"An increase in CO₂ caused less energy to flow out of the atmosphere, which raised temperatures."

The reverse (Jan 2026 Q47):
"Decreased CO₂ / lower levels of greenhouse gas CO₂" → "Earth's surface temperature decreased / the temperature was lower/cooler."
DO write: "CO₂ traps heat / less energy escapes the atmosphere → temperatures rise."
DON'T write: "CO₂ causes global warming." (too vague - no mechanism)
Photosynthesis & the Carbon Cycle
  • Trees/plants use photosynthesis to take in CO₂ and store carbon → reduces atmospheric CO₂
  • When forests burn: carbon stored in biomass is released as CO₂ back to atmosphere
  • Decomposition also releases CO₂ back to atmosphere
  • Ocean absorbs CO₂ from atmosphere (air-sea gas exchange)
Jun 2025 Q11 (rated correct): "Trees are growing, so they take in carbon dioxide during photosynthesis, which stores carbon in trees and soil, preventing it from entering the atmosphere as CO₂."
Always name the process as photosynthesis - not just "trees absorb CO₂."
Ocean Acidification - CO₂ Entering the Ocean
  • More atmospheric CO₂ → more CO₂ absorbed by ocean
  • CO₂ in ocean → carbonate ions (decrease)
  • Less carbonate ions → marine calcifiers (coral, shellfish) cannot build shells → populations decline
Aug 2025 Q48–Q49 (rated correct):
"Atmospheric CO₂ entering ocean: increase; Ocean carbonate ions: decrease."
"With less carbonate ions, marine organisms that need carbonate ions will have a harder time surviving because they need carbonate ions to make their shells."
Ice → Sea Level Feedback
  • Ice mass decreases → ice melts → sea level rises
  • Meltwater entering ocean → salinity decreases
  • Darker surface exposed when ice melts → more energy absorbed → positive feedback loop → more melting
Jan 2026 Q29 (rated correct): "As the ice mass decreased, it caused the hydrosphere (ocean) sea level to increase because the ice melted due to an increase in temperature." / "When ice melted, fresh water entered the ocean, causing the salinity to drop."
Obliquity (Earth's Axial Tilt) and Climate
  • Greater tilt (obliquity) → more extreme seasons → more energy to poles in summer
  • Smaller tilt → milder seasons → less energy to poles in winter → more ice forms
  • Current obliquity is 23.5°; minimum is 22.1° (decreasing over ~9,800 years)
At 22.1° obliquity in winter: northern hemisphere receives less energy → more ice forms in polar regions (Jun 2025 Q6 answer: choice 2).

Topic 3: Earth's History - Early Atmosphere & Life

Appears on ALL THREE exams
▼
The Great Oxidation Event - Master These Facts
  • ~2.4–2.1 billion years ago (Ga)
  • Cyanobacteria/stromatolites performed photosynthesis in the ocean
  • Oxygen released into ocean → eventually escaped to atmosphere
  • Oxygen reacted with methane → methane levels decreased
  • Oxygen levels increased in atmosphere
  • Result: less energy absorbed by atmosphere → Earth cooled → led to earliest ice ages (Huronian)
Jun 2025 Q30 (rated correct): "Microorganisms in the ocean produced oxygen through photosynthesis that then entered the atmosphere, causing increased weathering rates and allowing for the evolution of animal life."

Aug 2025 Q23 (rated correct): "Stromatolites carried out photosynthesis, which removed CO₂ from the ocean and atmosphere, and increased oxygen levels into the atmosphere."
Rating guide note: Responses must include the release of oxygen from the ocean by photosynthetic ocean organisms to the atmosphere. Simply saying "stromatolites made oxygen" is not enough.
Temporal Sequence of Earth Events - Know This Order

This exact sequence appeared as a question (Jan 2026 Q13). Memorize it:

  1. Formation of Earth (~4.6 Ga) - oldest
  2. First oceans form (~4.2 Ga)
  3. Iron enters ocean from black smokers (~4.0 Ga)
  4. First appearance of banded iron formations (~3.8 Ga)
  5. The Great Oxidation Event (~2.4–2.1 Ga) - youngest in this set
Additional markers: First evidence of life ~3.8 Ga; Cambrian explosion ~560 mya; Permian mass extinction ~250 mya.
Radioactive Dating - Half-Life and Age of Earth
  • Earth is approximately 4.5–4.6 billion years old
  • Uranium-238 half-life = 4.5 billion years
  • If ~50% of U-238 remains → sample is ~4.5 billion years old
  • Moon formed at approximately the same time as Earth (~4.5 Ga)
Jun 2025 Q28 (rated correct): "Based on the graph, the age of the meteorite is 4.5 billion years old, so Earth must also be almost 4.5 billion years old since they formed at about the same time."

Aug 2025 Q35 (rated correct): "Uranium-238 has a half-life of 4.5 billion years and Sample A has approximately half of its original uranium-238 remaining, which indicates the Moon is approximately 4.5 billion years old."
DO include: The half-life value AND the percentage remaining AND the conclusion about age.
DON'T: Just write the age without connecting it to the data.
Plate Tectonics & Rock Cycle - Cycling of Matter
  • Divergent boundaries → mid-ocean ridges → new ocean crust formed, magma rises
  • Convergent boundaries → subduction → oceanic plate sinks → magma forms → rises back to surface
  • Banded iron formations = evidence that iron + oxygen existed in ancient oceans → formed on ocean floor
  • Hotspots: plate moves over fixed hotspot → islands get older to the northwest in the Pacific
Jun 2025 Q47 - Hawaiian Islands (rated correct):
Pattern of ages: "The islands get older to the northwest" / "Islands get older with increasing distance from the hotspot."
Plate movement: "The Pacific Plate moves to the northwest."

Topic 4: Orbital Mechanics & Kepler's Laws

Appears on ALL THREE exams
▼
The Three Laws You Must Know Cold
LawWhat It SaysHow It Appears on Exams
Kepler's 1st Orbits are ellipses, with the Sun at one focus Eccentricity questions; eccentricity of 0 = perfect circle
Kepler's 2nd
(Equal Areas)		 An object sweeps equal areas in equal times → moves faster when closer "Mercury travels faster when closer to the Sun" ; tidal locking justification
Kepler's 3rd
(Orbital Period)		 Objects farther from the central body have longer orbital periods Ranking moons by period; comparing HST vs Moon orbital period
Jan 2026 Q19 justification (rated correct): "According to one of Kepler's laws, objects orbiting at a greater distance have a longer period of revolution." / "In order to sweep out equal area in equal time, moons travel faster when closer to a planet, causing a shorter orbital period for moons that are closer."
Common trap: "The orbital speeds of both planets are affected by their masses" - this is FALSE by Kepler's Laws. Speed depends on distance from the Sun, not the planet's own mass.
Tidal Locking - "Same Side Always Faces"

Tidal locking occurs when a moon's orbital period = its rotational period.

  • Earth's Moon: orbital period = 27.3 days, rotation = 27.3 days → same side always faces Earth
  • Charon (Pluto's moon): orbital period = 6.4 days, rotation = 6.4 days → same side always faces Pluto
Jan 2026 Q22 (rated correct - Support): "Charon's orbital period is 6.4 Earth days and its rotational period is also 6.4 Earth days."
Gravity & Orbital Speed
  • Closer to central body → stronger gravitational field → must orbit faster to stay in orbit
  • HST (540 km up) is much closer to Earth than the Moon → HST has a shorter orbital period
  • Gravitational field strength: g = GmE / r² → increases as distance (r) decreases
Aug 2025 Q14 passage completion (rated correct):
A: "less" (HST is less far than Moon)
B: "greater" (Earth's gravity at HST's location is greater)
C: "less" (HST has a shorter orbital period)

Topic 5: Water, Weathering & Erosion

Appears on ALL THREE exams
▼
Freeze-Thaw Weathering (Aug 2025)
  • Water expands by ~10% when it freezes
  • This expansion increases pressure in cracks → causes deformation/weathering of rock or concrete
  • Adding rebar to concrete reduces deformation (less cracking)
Aug 2025 Q6 (rated correct):
Property of water: "When water freezes, it expands by about 10%."
Relative amount: "Concrete samples with rebar have a lower deformation with fewer freeze-thaw cycles than concrete without rebar."
Erosion Rate - Sediment Size & Rock Resistance
  • Sandstone is more resistant to weathering/erosion than shale
  • Rock layers that extend farther out = more resistant (less eroded)
  • Shale shows more erosional cutback than sandstone
Jan 2026 Q38 (rated correct - Sandstone): "A sandstone layer is at the top of each waterfall, so it must be more resistant to weathering and erosion." / "The shale shows more erosional cutback than the sandstone."
Dams, Erosion & Sediment
  • Dams trap sediment behind them → less sediment reaches the coast
  • Less sediment at coast → coastline erosion / loss of beaches / loss of deltas
  • Diverting water away from falls → less water flowing over falls → slower erosion rate
Aug 2025 Q38 (rated correct): "Prior to the dam's construction, more sediment would have been deposited along the coast. After construction, more sediment is trapped behind the dam, reducing coastal deposition."

Aug 2025 Q9 (rated correct): "Less water flowing over Horseshoe Falls due to diversion above the falls resulted in a slower rate of erosion at the falls."
Plant Cover, Runoff & Soil Erosion
  • More plant cover → less runoff → less soil erosion
  • Less plant cover → more runoff → more soil erosion
Aug 2025 Q24 claim format: "As the percent of plant cover increases, runoff will decrease/become less." / "As the percent of plant cover decreases, the amount of soil eroded during a rain event will increase/become greater."
Temporal Scale of Geologic Processes

A favorite question type: comparing how fast different processes happen.

ProcessTimescale
Earthquake land subsidenceSeconds / minutes / hours (less than one day)
Ghost forest development (salt intrusion killing trees)Years / decades / centuries
Erosion at a waterfallYears to centuries
Plate movementMillions of years
Jun 2025 Q39 (rated correct): Earthquake land subsidence: "happens over seconds/minutes/hours." Ghost forest development: "happens over years/decades/centuries" / "occurs over much longer periods of time than earthquake sinking."
Saltwater Intrusion - What Gets Affected
  • Sea level rise → saltwater moves inland → contaminates freshwater aquifers and wells
  • Kills freshwater trees → loss of lumber (biosphere/geosphere impact)
  • Contaminates drinking water wells (hydrosphere impact)
Jun 2025 Q37 (rated correct - fresh water): "Salt water will intrude into the freshwater well, which would negatively impact the quality of drinking water and cause the resident to move their well."

(wood/timber): "The salt water intrusion would kill the trees in the freshwater forested wetland over time, which would reduce the supply of available lumber for building and heating."

Topic 6: Human Impact & Environmental Solutions

Appears on ALL THREE exams - usually involves comparing two design solutions
▼
Mining Impacts on Earth Systems
  • Mining requires land clearing/deforestation → more erosion of the geosphere
  • Deforestation → biodiversity of the biosphere reduced
  • Mine waste (tailings) stored on surface → precipitation dissolves materials → groundwater pollution (hydrosphere)
  • Airborne particles from tailings → air pollution (atmosphere)
Jan 2026 Q42 - two-part answer required (rated correct):
Negative effect: "Land must be cleared/deforested to dig a mine."
Earth system change: "When land is deforested, the biodiversity of the biosphere is reduced/eliminated."

OR: "Mine wastes (tailings) are stored on the surface" → "Precipitation dissolves materials in the mine waste, which infiltrates to groundwater, polluting it [hydrosphere]."
Carbon Emissions - Comparing Transportation / Energy Sources
  • Always use specific numbers when comparing carbon emissions
  • Battery Electric Vehicle: lowest total emissions (41 tCO₂e)
  • Hybrid Electric: 48 tCO₂e
  • Internal Combustion: 57 tCO₂e (highest)
  • Eurostar train: 6 g/km per passenger (lowest)
  • Ferry: 19 g/km; Short-haul flight: 255 g/km (highest)
Jun 2025 Q31 (rated correct - Battery Electric Vehicle): "Carbon dioxide is a greenhouse gas, and the total emissions for electric vehicles are 16 tCO₂e less than that of internal combustion engine vehicles."
DO: State the specific numbers. "The Eurostar uses 6 g/km vs 255 g/km for flights."
DON'T: Just say "electric vehicles pollute less." No numbers = no credit on numerical evidence questions.
Design Solution Questions - What Scorers Want

When comparing two solutions (e.g., river vs. ocean plastic removal; natural paving vs. recycled asphalt), the question usually asks for a benefit and a drawback or asks you to pick the better solution with evidence.

  • Read all the pros and cons in the provided table before answering
  • Your evidence must come from the provided data, not general knowledge
  • If asked about society's wants/needs: address both economic AND environmental aspects if the data shows both
Jun 2025 Q32 - Economic benefit + Society (rated correct):
Economic: "Class 1 and class 7 drivers that drive during off-peak hours with a Green Pass will save $3.50."
Society: "Class 1 and class 7 vehicles driven during off-peak hours with a Green Pass will emit less air pollution."
